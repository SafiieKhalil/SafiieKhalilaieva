Reading Reflection #4

W.J. Hennigan’s article “Nuclear War: The Rising Risk, and How We Stop It” presents a disturbing but important discussion about the growing dangers of nuclear conflict in the modern world. Before reading the article, I mostly associated nuclear war with the Cold War and believed it was less of a threat today. However, Hennigan explains that the risk has actually increased because of political tensions, modernization of nuclear weapons, cyber threats, and unstable international relationships. The article made me realize that nuclear war is not only a historical fear but also a current global issue that could affect everyone.

One of the most powerful aspects of the article is how it emphasizes the speed and unpredictability of nuclear conflict. Hennigan explains that leaders may have only a few minutes to respond to a possible attack, leaving almost no time for careful decision-making. This was shocking to me because it shows how human error, panic, or false information could lead to catastrophic consequences. The article highlights how dangerous it is for the world to depend on systems that require instant reactions in moments of extreme stress.

Another important point in the reading is the idea that nuclear weapons continue to exist because countries believe they provide security and power. However, Hennigan argues that these weapons also create constant fear and instability. Instead of guaranteeing safety, they increase the possibility of destruction on a massive scale. I found this contradiction especially interesting because it reflects how nations often prioritize military strength over long-term peace and cooperation.

The article also focuses on the human consequences of nuclear war. Beyond the political and military discussions, Hennigan reminds readers that millions of ordinary people would suffer the effects of radiation, famine, environmental collapse, and loss of life. Entire cities could be destroyed within minutes, and survivors would face unimaginable conditions. Reading this made the issue feel more personal and emotional rather than simply political. It is frightening to think that civilians who have no involvement in global conflicts would experience the greatest suffering.

I also appreciated that the article does not only describe the dangers but also discusses possible solutions. Hennigan stresses the importance of diplomacy, communication between nations, arms-control agreements, and reducing nuclear arsenals. These solutions may seem difficult, but the article suggests that international cooperation is necessary if humanity wants to avoid disaster. Public awareness is also important because many people rarely think about nuclear weapons even though they remain one of the greatest threats to global survival.

Overall, “Nuclear War: The Rising Risk, and How We Stop It” left a strong impression on me. The article is both informative and alarming because it explains how fragile global security truly is. Hennigan effectively combines political analysis with human concerns to show why nuclear war remains a serious modern danger. After reading the article, I feel more aware of the importance of peace, responsible leadership, and international cooperation. The reading serves as a warning that the world cannot afford to ignore the risks of nuclear weapons, especially in a time of increasing global tension.
